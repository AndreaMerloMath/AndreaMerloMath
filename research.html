The Parabolic Density Problem

Rectifiablity is the central concept of Geometric Measure Theory. Although the modern formal definition was given by H. Federer in the 60s (see [1]) the idea of relaxing the notion of smooth surface to that of countable union of Lipschitz images was already present in the seminal works [2–4] of A. Besicovitch. Henceforth, the characterization of rectifiability via both analytic and geometric properties has produced an extensive amount of research. We refer for instance to the books [1], [5], the recent survey [6], and the references therein.

Since Besicovitch’s foundational works, one of the key problems of Geometric Measure Theory was to determine the geometric structure of measures with density, namely Radon measures \(\phi\) on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) such that the limit \[\lim_{r\to 0}\frac{\phi(B(x,r))}{r^\alpha} \label{BP}\] exists positive and finite for some \(\alpha>0\) and for \(\phi\)-almost every \(x\in \mathbb{R}^n\). This query, usually referred to as the density problem, has driven a lot of research during the 60s and 70s (see for instance [7, 8]), and was finally solved in the celebrated D. Preiss’s work [9]. Its relevance is even broader, since it led the main contributors to its solution in \(\mathbb R^n\), namely A. Besicovitch, J. Marstrand, P. Mattila, and D. Preiss, to develop many of the tools, blowup analysis arguments, and results that became fundamental techniques in the study of local properties of measures.

The solution of the Euclidean density problem is summarised in the following theorem, which is commonly known as Preiss’s rectifiability criterion.

Let \(\phi\) be a Radon measure on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and let \(\alpha\geq 0\). The following statements are equivalent:

  • \(\alpha\in\{0,1,\ldots,n\}\), \(\phi\) is absolutely continuous with respect to the Hausdorff measure \(\mathcal{H}^{\alpha}\), and \(\phi\)-almost all of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) can be covered with countably many Lipschitz images of \(\mathbb R^\alpha.\)

  • The limit in [BP] exists positive and finite \(\phi\)-almost everywhere, where \(B(x,r)\) is the Euclidean ball of centre \(x\) and radius \(r>0\).

As mentioned above, the proof of Theorem [preiss] was a community effort. The case \(n=2\) was completely solved by Besicovitch in [2–4]. Marstrand proved in [7, 10] that if [BP] exists positive and finite in an arbitrary dimension \(n\), then \(\alpha\) has to be an integer. He further showed that Theorem [preiss] holds for \(n=3\) and \(\alpha=2\). Ten years later, Mattila proved in [8] that if \(E\subset \mathbb R^n\) is measurable, \(\phi=\mathcal{H}^\alpha\llcorner E\), and the limit in [BP] equals \(1\) \(\mathcal{H}^\alpha\llcorner E\)-almost everywhere, then \(E\) must be rectifiable. See also [11]. In order to achieve this result, Mattila obtained in full generality another keystone of Geometric Measure Theory which is often referred to as Marstrand-Mattila rectifiability criterion and allows one to infer the global property of rectifiability of a measure from a pointwise information which involves proper flatness conditions. We refer the reader to [12] for more details and a proof. Finally, Preiss’s contribution to Theorem [preiss] was to find a way to link the information given by the existence of [BP] to the local regularity and flatness of the measure \(\phi\). The importance of [9] further stems from the fundamental techniques which were introduced there, such as the theory of tangent measures. These methods played a crucial role also in many modern applications such as the study of harmonic measure in [13–17].

Because of the wide range of possible applications in the theory of optimal transport and PDEs, the last twenty years have witnessed a remarkable surge in interest for an extension of the results of Geometric Measure Theory in the context of metric spaces. For some relevant examples we refer to [18–21]. A family of metric spaces which has received a special attention are Carnot groups, namely simply connected nilpotent Lie group whose Lie algebra is stratified and generated by its first layer (see for instance [22]). Their main relevance is twofold: first, they are infinitesimal models of geodesic metric spaces (see [23]) and, secondly, all non-commutative Carnot groups are \(1\) and \(2\)-codimensional purely unrectifiable metric spaces, in the sense that they do not contain Lipschitz images of codimension \(1\) or \(2\). For the latter reason it is apparent that if one wants to extend the geometric techniques to these settings, at least in codimension \(1\), the classical theory is of no help.

A significant step toward a better understanding of rectifiability in Carnot groups was made in the early 2000’s by B. Franchi, R. Serapioni, and F. Serra Cassano, who introduced in [24] an intrinsic notion of rectifiability in the Heisenberg group and achieved an analogue of De Giorgi’s rectifiability theorem. In particular, they formulated it in terms of so-called \(\mathcal C^1_{\mathbb H}\)-regular surface (see [24, Definition 6.1]), which admit an associated Implicit Function Theorem (see [24, Theorem 6.5]). The first named author has recently proved in [25] and [26] that \(\mathcal C^1_{\mathbb H}\)-rectifiability is indeed the correct framework to provide the first non-Euclidean analogue of Preiss’s theorem. His result was attained for 1-codimensional measures in \(\mathbb H^n\). Motivated by [26], he has also formulated a notion of \(\mathcal P^*\)-rectifiability (resp. \(\mathcal P\)-rectifiability) in general Carnot groups which is based on the almost everywhere flatness (resp. flatness ) of tangent measures, which he further investigated in collaboration with G. Antonelli in [27], [28], and [29]. As a consequence of the works [27], [30], [31], [25], [26] of G. Antonelli, V. Chousionis, V. Magnani, J. Tyson, and the first named author, Preiss’s density theorem is also known to hold in \(\mathbb H^1\) for higher codimensional measures. For a statement and a more detailed discussion we refer to the introduction of [27].

In Euclidean spaces, the different characterizations of (uniformly) rectifiable measures provide multiple equivalent definitions that can be used to solve a wide range of deep problems in PDEs, harmonic analysis, and calculus of variations. However, in the parabolic setting, there has been a much more limited progress on developing equivalent characterizations of rectifiability. As a result, there is a substantial lack of flexibility in applying geometric measure theory to parabolic problems.

Our work aims to address this issue by providing the first equivalent characterization of rectifiability in parabolic spaces. The ultimate goal is to relate qualitative and quantitative definitions in the spirit, just to mention a few examples, of [32–35]. Specifically, we investigate the relationship between the densities, tangents, and regularity of measures in the non-Euclidean setting of parabolic spaces \(\mathbb{P}^n\) (see §[parabgroup] for the definition), with the goal of solving the density problem for \(1\)-codimensional measures on \(\mathbb{P}^n\). Throughout our investigation, we also develop several fundamental tools in Geometric Measure Theory adapted to the parabolic space.

Furthermore, we provide the first equivalent characterization of parabolic rectifiability and try to bridge the qualitative and quantitative definitions. In particular, we investigate the relationship between densities, tangents, and regularity of measures in parabolic spaces \(\mathbb{P}^n\) (see §[parabgroup] for the definition), to solve the density problem.

Before delving into the explanation of our contributions to the matter, we give a short account of what has been done so far in the parabolic spaces. Motivated by the study the heat equation on time-varying domains, S. Hofmann, J. Lewis, and K. Nyström introduced in [36] and [37] a definition of quantitative (uniform) \((n+1)\)-rectifiability in the parabolic space, which is inspired by the works of David and Semmes’ in the Euclidean case (see [38] and [39]). Its investigation constitutes an active field of research (see [40–42]). We also mention that the need of a better understanding of qualitative parabolic rectifiability arises also from other recent studies beyond geometry. Parabolic tangent measures and some of the techniques of [9] have been implemented in the study of non-variational two-phase problems for caloric measure by the second and third named author in [43]. Moreover, J. Mateu, L. Prat, and X. Tolsa investigated in [44] a parabolic Lipschitz-harmonic capacity in the context of removability for Lipschitz caloric functions.

We now proceed with a description of the content of the paper and its connection with the existing literature. As mentioned above, the main subject is the density problem in \(\mathbb{P}^n\), which we solve in codimension \(1\).

Suppose that \(\phi\) is a Radon measure on \(\mathbb{P}^n\). Then, the following are equivalent:

  • \(\phi\) is absolutely continuous with respect to \(\mathcal{H}^{n+1}\) and it is \(\mathscr{P}_{n+1}\)-rectifiable in the sense of Definition [def:PhRectifiableMeasure]. In other words, there are countably many compact sets \(K_i\subseteq \mathcal{V}\) and Lipschitz maps \(g_i\colon K_i\to \mathbb{R}\) with the Rademacher property (see Definition [def:Rademacher_property]) such that \[\phi\Bigl(\mathbb{P}^n\setminus \bigcup_{i\in\mathbb{N}} g_i(K_i)\Bigr)=0.\]

  • \(\phi\) is absolutely continuous with respect to \(\mathcal{H}^{n+1}\) and it is concentrated on a \(1\)-codimensional LG-rectifiable set.

  • For \(\phi\)-almost every \(x\in\mathbb{P}^n\) we have \[0<\lim_{r\to 0}\frac{\phi(B(x,r))}{r^{n+1}}<\infty,\] where \(B(x,r)\) is the ball relative to the Koranyi metric (see [eq:Korany_metric]).

In [45], P. Mattila introduced the notion of LG-rectifiabile set in the spirit of Federer’s definition (see Definition [def:LGrect]), which allowed him to provide the analogues of the classical rectifiability and structural results for such sets. It is easily seen that LG-rectifiability is equivalent to item (i) of the theorem above. Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss] is a parabolic analogue of Theorem [preiss] and of [25, Theorem 1.3]; we show that Mattila’s notion of LG-rectifiability is precisely the one which solves the \(1\)-codimensional density problem in \(\mathbb{P}^n\) for the Koranyi metric. This is quite remarkable both from the theoretical point of view, as it provides a metric reason why the notion of LG-rectifiability is natural and, on the other hand, together with results like [45, Example 8.2], it highlights a contrast between the notion of parabolic uniform rectifiability and the geometry of the parabolic space. We shall discuss the latter issue in detail below. This completely solves, in the case of codimension \(1\), the question posed by Mattila in [45] as to whether an analogue of Preiss’s theorem can hold in \(\mathbb{P}^n\).

It is legitimate to ask whether Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss] keeps being true if we replace the Koranyi norm with a bi-Lipschitz equivalent one. The next result shows that the solution to the density problem in the parabolic space is extremely sensitive to the change of norms. Indeed, in Appendix [section:counterexample_PDT] we provide a counterexample to the parabolic Preiss’s density theorem with an alternative metric.

Endow the parabolic group \(\mathbb{P}^1\) with the metric induced by the norm 1 \[\|x\|_\infty \mathrm{:=} \max\{|x_H|, |x_T|^{1/2}\} \qquad \text{ for }\quad x=(x_H,x_T)\in \mathbb{R}\times \mathbb{R} \label{infinitynorm}\] and let \(\mathfrak B_\infty(x,r)\) denote the ball relative to [infinitynorm]. Then, there exists a Radon measure \(\nu\) on \(\mathbb{P}^1\) which satisfies \[\nu(\mathfrak B_\infty(x,r))=r^2\qquad \text{for any $x\in\mathrm{supp}(\nu)$ and any $r>0$}\] and such that the set of Preiss’s tangent measures \(\mathrm{Tan}_2(\nu,x)\) (see Definition [def:TangentMeasure]) for \(\nu\)-almost every \(x\in\mathbb{P}^1\) never contains the Haar measure of the vertical line \(\mathcal V\mathrm{:=} \{(0,s):s\in\mathbb{R}\}\).

It is not hard to show that \(\mathcal V\) is the only \(2\)-dimensional homogeneous subgroup of \(\mathbb{P}^1\) (see Corollary [corollary:grn1]). Thus, Theorem [th:failurepreiss] proves the existence of a Radon measure having \(2\)-dimensional density with respect to the standard parabolic cylinder \(\mathfrak B_\infty(x,r)\) such that \(\nu\) never resembles a flat parabolic surface at any scale and at \(\nu\)-almost every point of \(\mathbb{P}^1\). Together with Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss], the above results also prove that \(\mathbb{P}^n\) is a metric space for which two bi-Lipschitz equivalent metrics give different solutions to the density problem, and, to our knowledge, this is the first instance where such a phenomenon has ever been observed. We also mention that Theorem [th:failurepreiss] shows one of the reasons why it is so difficult to prove rectifiability criteria in the spirit of Theorem [preiss]: the class of measures for which the density exists depends on the metric, so all the arguments must heavily rely on the shape of the ball. Let us highlight that Theorem [th:failurepreiss] shows a new phenomenon. Indeed, even in \(\ell^3_\infty\) A. Lorent showed in [46] that \(2\)-dimensional uniform measures are rectifiable. However, in \(\ell^3_\infty\) it is still open if Preiss’s rectifiability theorem extends in codimension \(1\).

Let us discuss the strategy of the proof of Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss], which can be divided into two main steps. The first is to obtain an analogue of Marstrand-Mattila rectifiability criterion in \(\mathbb{P}^n\), which holds for general homogeneous (with respect to parabolic dilations, see [eq:homogeous_metric]) and translation-invariant metrics. This links the local structure of a measure to its global regularity properties. We further remark that this type of results is extremely useful in applications since, instead of having to check a strong rigidity like the uniqueness and flatness of the blowups, it is sufficient to prove that, locally, the measure is geometrically flat although at different scales its tangent might rotate.

Let \(\mathfrak d\) be a homogeneous metric on \(\mathbb P^n\) which is invariant under translations, see [eq:homogeous_metric] and [eq:transl_inv_d]. Let \(\phi\) be a Radon measure on \((\mathbb{P}^{n}, \mathfrak d)\) such that:

  • There exists \(h\in\{0,\ldots,n+2\}\) such that for \(\phi\)-almost every \(x\in \mathbb{P}^n\) we have \[0< % \Theta^h_*(\phi,x)\mathrm{:=} \liminf_{r\to 0}\frac{\phi(B_{\mathfrak d}(x,r))}{r^{h}}\leq\limsup_{r\to 0}\frac{\phi(B_{\mathfrak d}(x,r))}{r^{h}}, % \eqqcolon \Theta^{h,*}(\phi,x) <\infty,\] where \(B_{\mathfrak d}(x,r)\mathrm{:=} \{y\in \mathbb P^n:\mathfrak d(x,y)\leq r\}\).

  • For \(\phi\)-almost every \(x\in \mathbb{P}^n\) the elements of \(\mathrm{Tan}_h(\phi,x)\) contained in the family of \(h\)-dimensional flat measures \(\mathfrak{M}(h)\), namely the family of Haar measures relative to \(h\)-dimensional homogeneous subgroups of \(\mathbb{P}^n\).

Then there are countably many \(h\)-dimensional homogeneous subgroups \(W_i\) of \(\mathbb{P}^n\), compact sets \(K_i\subset W_i\), and differentiable Lipschitz maps \(f_i\colon K_i\to \mathbb{P}^n\) such that \[\phi\Bigl(\mathbb{P}^n\setminus \bigcup_{i\in\mathbb{N}} f_i(K_i)\Bigr)=0.\]

With the above theorem in hand, in Proposition [preiss1] we prove Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss] in the case \(n=1\). Theorem [thm:MMconormale:intro] tells us that if a measure has dimension \(h\), which is item (i), and it is well approximated by planes at small scales, which is item (ii), then it essentially is the surface measure of a differentiable Lipschitz graph of dimension \(h\). We shall remark, however, that in the statement of Theorem [thm:MMconormale:intro] we allow the plane which approximates the measure to possibly change at different scales, in a similar fashion to what happens at the centre of an exponential spiral. Hence, to prove rectifiability with such weak hypotheses is significantly harder than in presence of a unique blowup, which is commonly referred to as the characterisation in terms of approximate tangent plane (see [1]).

The first step to achieve Theorem [thm:MMconormale:intro] is to show that items (i) and (ii) imply that \(\mathbb{P}^n\) can be covered \(\phi\)-almost all by countably many Lipschitz images of compact subsets of an \(h\)-dimensional homogeneous subgroup of \(\mathbb{P}^n\). To do this, we use techniques analogous to those that Federer developed to obtain his celebrated projection theorem (see [47]). In order to prove that the Lipschitz graphs \(\Gamma_i\) previously constructed are differentiable, we show that the locality of tangent measures given by Proposition [tuttitg] yields that the tangents relative to the natural surface measures of \(\Gamma_i\) are flat almost everywhere. The proof that such blowup is unique is a (much) more delicate version of the uniqueness of the differential (see Proposition [mm0.0]).

If not specified, throughout the rest of this introductory section we always understand that \(\mathbb{P}^n\) is endowed with the Koranyi metric.

The second step to prove Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss] also in the case \(n>1\) can be summarized with the following crucial result, which shows that a measure in the parabolic space with \(1\)-codimensional density has flat tangents almost everywhere.

Suppose that \(\phi\) is a Radon measure on \(\mathbb P^n\) such that \[0<\lim_{r\to 0}\frac{\phi(B(x,r))}{r^{n+1}}<\infty,\qquad\text{for $\phi$-almost every $x\in\mathbb{P}^n$. } \label{eq:BPdensity}\] Then item (ii) in Theorem [thm:MMconormale:intro] holds true.

To prove that (iii) implies (i) in Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss] it is enough to combine Theorems [thm:MMconormale:intro] and [preissprabolicointrovero], while the other implications follow almost immediately from Mattila’s work [45]. Theorem [preissprabolicointrovero] is the technical core of the paper and the results employed in its proof have several by-products, as we detail below. The main idea of its proof is to exploit the weak* connectedness of \(\mathrm{Tan}_{n+1}(\mu,x)\) and show the following result.

Let us recall that a Radon measures \(\nu\) on \(\mathbb{P}^n\) is called \((n+1)\)-uniform if \(\nu(B(x,r))=r^{n+1}\) for any \(x\in\mathrm{supp}(\nu)\) and any \(r>0\).

Let \(\phi\) be a Radon measure on \(\mathbb P^n\) such that \[0<\lim_{r\to 0}\frac{\phi(B(x,r))}{r^{n+1}}<\infty\qquad \text{for $\phi$-almost every $x\in\mathbb{P}^n$}.\] Suppose further that

  • there exist a weak* continuous functional \(\mathscr{F}\) on the space of Radon measures and a constant \(\varepsilon>0\) depending only on \(n\) such that, if \(\mu\) is an \((n+1)\)-uniform measure on \(\mathbb P^n\) and \(\mathscr{F}(\nu)\leq\varepsilon\) for some tangent at infinity \(\nu\) of \(\mu\) (see Definition [def:blowdowns]), then \(\mu\) is flat.

Then item (ii) in Theorem [thm:MMconormale:intro] holds true.

It is easy to see that if a measure satisfies [eq:BPdensity], then its tangent measures are uniform at \(\phi\)-almost every point. This information is not sufficient to conclude a priori that [eq:BPdensity] implies the flatness of blowups. Indeed, it is very well known that there are uniform measures in \(\mathbb{R}^n\) which are not flat (see for instance [48] and [49]) so the proof of Theorem [preissprabolicointrovero] requires a finer understanding of the geometric structure of uniform measures.

Condition (P) should be interpreted as the property that the class of \(1\)-codimensional uniform measures can be split into flat and non-flat measures, and those two families are weak* disconnected. Moreover, the fact that \(\mathrm{Tan}_{n+1}(\phi,x)\) is weak* connected for \(\phi\)-almost every \(x\in\mathbb P^n\) implies that either the measures in \(\mathrm{Tan}_{n+1}(\phi,x)\) are flat or \(\mathrm{Tan}_{n+1}(\phi,x)\) is contained in the set of non-flat uniform measures \(\phi\)-almost everywhere. However, since uniform measures are surface measures of \(1\)-codimensional analytic varieties in \(\mathbb{R}^{n+1}\) and thanks to the fact that the tangent measures of a tangent measure are themselves tangent measures by Proposition [tuttitg], we infer that \(\mathrm{Tan}_{n+1}(\phi,x)\) contains a flat measure for \(\phi\)-almost every \(x\in \mathbb P^n\). The above argument shows that item (ii) of Theorem [thm:MMconormale:intro] holds.

Given the close relation of the spaces \(\mathbb{H}^n\) and \(\mathbb{P}^n\), one could expect that either the strategy to prove (P) is quite similar in the two cases or that the property in the parabolic space can be derived from the proof in Heisenberg groups. However, this is far from being true and the proof of (P) turned out to be extremely technical.

The first step in order to deduce (P) is to get some weak geometric information on the support of uniform measures. We follow the strategy of [9] and study the so-called moments (see Section [section:moments_expansion]), substituting the scalar product with a suitable polarization of the norm. We obtain the following result.

Let \(\nu\) be a \(1\)-codimensional uniform measure on \(\mathbb{P}^n\). Then either there are \(b\in\mathbb{R}^n\), \(\tau\in\mathbb{R}\), and a symmetric non-zero matrix \(\mathcal Q\in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}\) such that \[\mathrm{supp}(\mu)\subseteq \bigl\{x\in\mathbb{P}^n:\langle x_H,\mathcal Q[x_H]+b\rangle+\tau x_T=0\bigr\}, \label{supportquadratiic}\] or there exists \(C(n+1)>0\) such that \[\begin{split} 0=\lim_{s\to 0}&\frac{8 s^{\frac{3}{2}+\frac{n+1}{4}}}{C(n+1)}\int \lvert z_H\rvert^4 z_H\otimes z_H e^{-s\lVert z\rVert^4}\,d\mu(z)\\ &\qquad-\frac{s^{\frac{1}{2}+\frac{n+1}{4}}}{C(n+1)}\int (4z_H\otimes z_H+2\lvert z_H\rvert^2\textrm{id}_{n}) e^{-s\lVert z\rVert^4}\, d\mu(z). \label{eq:degeneratemeasures} \end{split}\]

Theorem [theorem:1_codim_unif_measures] shows how far the geometry of the parabolic space is from that of \(\mathbb R^n\) and \(\mathbb H^n\). Indeed, the solutions of the Euclidean and Heisenberg density problems heavily rely on the rigidity which follows from the fact that, in those spaces, uniform measures are concentrated on the zero set of quadratic polynomials (see [25] and [9]). However, we have not been able to show any analogue of such result in \(\mathbb{P}^n\).

This forced our approach to diverge significantly from those previously employed. As seen above, in order to prove Theorem [preissprabolicointrovero] we just need to show that, inside the set of uniform measures, flat measures are weak* disconnected from the non-flat ones. Let \(\mathscr{F}\colon \mathcal{M}\to\mathbb{R}^+\) be the functional \[\label{eq:functional_F_intro} \mathscr{F}(\nu)\mathrm{:=}\inf_{u\in\mathbb{S}^{n-1}} \int \lvert z_H\rvert^4\langle z_H,u\rangle^2 e^{-\lVert z\rVert^4}\, d\nu(z).\] A relatively standard computation proves that \(\mathscr{F}\) disconnects \(1\)-codimensional uniform measures satisfying [eq:degeneratemeasures] from the flat ones (see Proposition [disconnectdegenerated]). The most complex task is then to discuss the geometry of uniform measures concentrated on quadrics, which one can show are surface measures associated to the quadratic surfaces. Moreover, thanks to a hard-won Taylor expansion of the perimeter measure together with some delicate algebraic considerations (see Appendix [TYLR] and Subsection [sectneq0notexist] respectively) we also deduce that the quadrics that can support a uniform measure must be vertically ruled. Because of this reduction and the coarea-type formula in Proposition [prop:reprez], we obtain the following result.

Let \(\mu\) be an \((n+1)\)-uniform measure on \(\mathbb P^n\). If \(\mu\) is concentrated on a quadric, then either \(\nu\) is flat or up to isometries there exists \(c=c(n)>0\) such that \[\mu=c\mathcal{H}^3\llcorner \{x_1^2+x^2_2+x_3^2=x^4\}\otimes \mathcal{L}^{n-4}\llcorner\mathrm{span}\{e_4,\ldots,e_n\}\otimes \mathcal{L}^1\llcorner \mathrm{span}\{e_{n+1}\}.\]

If, on the other hand, \(\mu\) is not concentrated on a quadratic surface, there exists a constant \(\oldep{eps:1}=\oldep{eps:1}(n+1)>0\) such that \[\nonumber \lambda^{\frac{3}{2}+\frac{n+1}{4}}\int \lvert z_H\rvert^4\langle z_H,u\rangle^2 e^{-\lambda\lVert z\rVert^4}\, d\nu(z)>\oldep{eps:1}\] for any \(\nu\in \mathrm{Tan}_{n+1}(\mu,\infty)\), any \(\lambda>0\) and any \(u\in\mathbb{R}^n\) with \(\lvert u\rvert=1\).

Theorem [classificazionemisureunif] implies property (P), concluding the proof of Theorem [preissprabolicointrovero] and hence of Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss]. We also remark that we actually prove an analogue of the second part of Theorem [classificazionemisureunif] for uniform measures of arbitrary codimension.

The above characterisation of uniform measures is the key tool to obtain the results outlined in the rest of the introduction, where we present some applications to the study of parabolic uniform rectifiability.

A Radon measure \(\mu\) on \(\mathbb P^n\) is said \((n+1)\)-Ahlfors regular, or simply \((n+1)\)-Ahlfors-regular, if there exists \(C>0\) such that \[C^{-1}r^{n+1}\leq \mu(B(x,r))\leq C r^{n+1}\] for all \(x\in \mathrm{supp}(\mu)\) and \(0<r<\mathrm{diam} (\mathrm{supp}(\mu)).\) As previously mentioned, Hofmann, Lewis, and Nyström proposed a parabolic equivalent of David and Semmes’ notion of uniform rectifiability of a set \(\Sigma \subset \mathbb P^n\). They formulated it in terms of a Carleson condition on the measure \[d\nu(x,r)=\beta_{2,\sigma_\Sigma}(x,r)\,d\sigma_\Sigma(x)\,r^{-1}dr\] on \(\mathbb P^n\times \mathbb R_{>0}\), where \(\sigma_\Sigma\) is the surface measure on \(\Sigma\) and \(\beta_{2,\sigma_\Sigma}\) are the (parabolic) \(\beta_{2}\)-numbers which quantify the flatness of \({\sigma_\Sigma}\). A crucial role in the study of parabolic uniform rectifiability is played by regular parabolic Lipschitz functions, namely maps \(f\colon \mathbb P^{n-1}\to \mathbb R\) which are Lipschitz with respect to the usual \(\ell^2\)-norm in the space variables and such that \(D^{1/2}_Tf\in \mathrm{BMO}(\mathbb P^{n-1})\), where \(D_T^{1/2}\) stands for the \(1/2\)-order derivative in the time variable and \(\mathrm{BMO}(\mathbb P^{n-1})\) for the parabolic version of the space of functions with bounded mean oscillation. Furthermore, Hofmann, Lewis, and Nyström proved that a parabolic uniformly rectifiable set which is also Reifenberg flat (in a parabolic sense) has big pieces of regular parabolic Lipschitz graphs. More recently it has been shown in [41] and [42] that a parabolic \((n+1)\)-Ahlfors regular \(\Sigma\) is parabolic uniformly rectifiabile if and only if it admits a (bilateral) corona decomposition with respect to regular parabolic Lipschitz graphs or, equivalently, if \(\Sigma\) has big pieces squared of regular parabolic Lispchitz graphs. For the precise definitions and a more in-depth discussion of these results we refer to the aforementioned papers.

Several different characterizations of Euclidean uniform rectifiability are available in the literature. For instance, it is well known that an \(n\)-Ahlfors regular measure on \(\mathbb R^{n+1}\) is uniformly rectifiable if and only if it satisfies the so-called bilateral weak geometric lemma (BWGL) (see the monograph [39]). However, it has been shown in [42, Observation 4.19] that the BWGL in the context of the parabolic space does not imply parabolic uniform rectifiability. As an application of our analysis of parabolic uniform measures, we prove that the parabolic weak constant density condition (WCD) implies the BWGL (see Definitions [def-WCD] and [def:BWGL] respectively). The precise definitions involve Jones’ \(\beta\)-numbers and the lattice of dyadic cubes adapted to a regular measure so, in order not to make the introduction too lengthy, we choose to defer them until Section [section:BWGL].

Let \(\mu\) be an \((n+1)\)-Ahlfors regular measure on \(\mathbb P^n\), and assume that it satisfies the weak constant density condition. Then \(\mu\) satisfies the bilateral weak geometric lemma.

The Euclidean analogue of Theorem [theorem:WCD_implies_BWGL_2] was first proved by David and Semmes in [39]. In the parabolic case, it follows from Theorem [classificazionemisureunif], or more precisely from Lemma [lemmaF->beta], together with a fine study of the flatness properties of uniform measures in the spirit of the arguments of Tolsa in [50]. We remark that the approach in [50] uses the Riesz transform to prove that if a uniform measure is far from being flat (respectively very flat) inside a cube \(Q\) then it is far from being flat (respectively very flat) in most of the subcubes of \(Q\). However, in \(\mathbb{P}^n\) the Riesz transform is not the right operator (compare with Proposition [prop:propaux1_s]) and in order to prove these strong propagation properties of flatness, we need to investigate more carefully the geometric structure of uniform measures (see Lemma [lemmaangolitangenti]).

Theorem [theorem:WCD_implies_BWGL_2] provides a direct link with the upcoming article [51] in which we exhibit further applications of the parabolic Preiss’s theory to the study the parabolic analogue of a proper quantitative version of the parabolic density problem. Hence, we find it useful to briefly outline some of its contents to put the result in a wider context. In [51] we are interested in establishing whether the characterisation given by Chousionis, Garnett, Le, and Tolsa of uniform rectifiability in terms of the boundedness of a proper square function involving the density given in [52] can be extended to the parabolic spaces. In particular, using the results of the present paper, we are able to prove the following result.

Suppose \(\mu\) is an \((n+1)\)-Ahlfors-regular measure on \(\mathbb{P}^n\) such that \[\int_{B(y,R)}\int_0^R\Big\lvert\frac{\mu(B(x,r))}{r^{n+1}}-\frac{\mu(B(x,2r))}{2^{n+1}r^{n+1}}\Big\rvert^{2}\,\frac{dr}{r}d\mu(x)\leq CR^{n+1} ,\quad \text{ for $\mu$-almost every $y\in\mathbb{P}^n$.} \label{sfe}\] Then \(\mu\) is \(\mathscr{P}_{n+1}\)-rectifiable in the sense of Definition [def:PhRectifiableMeasure] and it satisfies the WCD. In particular, \(\mu\) satisfies the BWGL by Theorem [theorem:WCD_implies_BWGL_2].

Theorem [densitythBWGL] is in line with what happens in Euclidean space although, as mentioned above, we cannot push the regularity given by the BWGL to parabolic uniform rectifiability. The following result, however, highlights profound differences in the parabolic setting.

There exists a \(2\)-Ahlfors-regular measure \(\mu\) on \(\mathbb{P}^1\) such that for any \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists a constant \(C_\varepsilon>0\) such that \[\int_0^1\Big\lvert\frac{\mu(B(x,r))}{r^{2}}-\frac{\mu(B(x,2r))}{4r^{2}}\Big\rvert^{\frac{1}{2}+\varepsilon}\,\frac{dr}{r}\leq C_\varepsilon,\qquad \text{ for $\mu$-almost every $x\in \mathbb{P}^n$,} \label{eq:density}\] and for any regular parabolic Lipschitz graph \(\Gamma\) we have \(\mu( \Gamma)=0\).

The idea behind Theorem [counterexamples:intro] is to construct a parabolic Lipschitz function \(f\colon\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) such that, if \(\Gamma\) denotes its graph, then [eq:density] holds for \(\mu=\mathcal{H}^2\llcorner \Gamma\) and \[\int_\mathbb{R}\int_\mathbb{R}\frac{\lvert f(\tau)-f(\sigma)\rvert^2}{\lvert \tau-\sigma\rvert^2}\,d\tau d\sigma=\infty. \label{eq.parabolicpip}\] Our construction is inspired by the one of non-differentiable continuous Weierstrass function and the techniques used in [45, Example 8.2], and it involves delicate Fourier estimates for the co-Lipschitz constant of the function \(f\). In fact, it gives much more than [eq.parabolicpip]. We finally mention that the proof of Theorem [counterexamples:intro] also requires a computation of the Hausdorff measure of balls centred on \(\Gamma\), which we achieve via a version of the area formula for parabolic differentiable Lipschitz functions which is of interest on it own right (see for instance [20, 28, 53]).

Structure of the paper.

Section [section:Preliminaries] collects preliminary facts and known results on general measure theory, uniform measures, and parabolic rectifiability. In Section [section:Mastrand] we prove some additional structural properties of uniform measures. V. Chousionis and J. Tyson showed in [30] that the support of a uniform measure on \(\mathbb P^n\) is an analytic variety, but we actually need more information; an adaptation of the techniques of [54] shows that a uniform measure actually coincides, modulo a dimensional factor, with the surface measure on its support. We further prove that the tangent measures to a uniform measure of codimension \(1\) are indeed dilation-invariant, and this analysis allows us to conclude the section with the classification of uniform measures on \(\mathbb P^1\) (see Proposition [uniformmeasuresinP1]).

Section [section:MM_rect_theorem] is devoted to the proof of Theorem [thm:MMconormale:intro]. In Section [section:moments_expansion] we introduce the parabolic version of Preiss’s moments associated with a uniform measure on \(\mathbb P^n\). This is done in Definition [defimom] and it involves a polarization of Koranyi norm (see Proposition [prop6]). Although most of the calculations mimics those in [9] and [25], this is a pivotal component of the proof since it allows to construct a candidate for an algebraic surface which contains the support of the uniform measure (see Section [subsection:expansion_moments_candidate_quadric]).

Section [section:unif_measure_quadratic_surface] contains the proof of Theorem [theorem:1_codim_unif_measures]: we split the class of \((n+1)\)-uniform measures into “non-degenerate” and “degenerate” measures (see [eq:degenerate]), prove that the first ones are contained in quadratic surfaces (see Proposition [prop:notdeg]), and finally show that [eq:degeneratemeasures] and [eq:prop47_statement] hold for the second sub-class. The analysis of non-degenerate \(1\)-codimensional uniform measures is continued in Section [eq:sec_non_deg_unif_meas], where we prove the first part of Theorem [classificazionemisureunif]. Then, in Section [section:proof_main_theorem] we study the properties of the functional \(\mathscr F\) in [eq:functional_F_intro] and conclude the proof of Theorem [theorem:main_theorem_Preiss].

In Section [section:BWGL] we apply the methods described above and prove Theorem [theorem:WCD_implies_BWGL_2]. The argument is based on a touching-point argument in terms of an auxiliary operator defined in [eq:definition_Rrs] which replaces the Riesz transform and allows us to infer the flatness result Corollary [corollary:flat_ball_big]. Moreover, we remark that the solution of the parabolic Preiss’s density problem is the key tool used in the form of Lemma [lemmaF->beta], and the proof of the BWGL follows from a stopping-time argument along the lines of [50].

Appendix [section:counterexample_PDT] contains the construction of the graph which proves Theorem [th:failurepreiss] and, finally, in Appendix [TYLR] we adapt the calculations in [25] in order to study the first three non-trivial coefficients of the expansion [eq:expansion_area_formula] of the Hausdorff measure on a quadratic surface.
